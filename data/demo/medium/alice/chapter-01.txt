# Alice's Adventures in Wonderland — Chapter I: Down the Rabbit-Hole (Public Domain)

Alice was beginning to get very tired of sitting by her sister on the bank,
and of having nothing to do: once or twice she had peeped into the book her
sister was reading, but it had no pictures or conversations in it, "and what
is the use of a book," thought Alice "without pictures or conversation?"

The afternoon was warm and still, with hardly a breath of wind stirring
the reeds along the riverbank. On the opposite shore, a flock of sheep
grazed lazily in a meadow of deep green grass. Overhead, a lark sang
from a height so great that she could scarcely see it. A butterfly,
blue and gold, fluttered past her nose and vanished among the rushes.
A fish jumped in the river, leaving a ring of ripples that widened and
faded. Alice sighed, shifted her position on the bank, and wished that
something—anything—would happen to break the monotony of the long, dull
day. She tried counting the daisies at her feet, but there were too many,
and they all looked alike. She tried making up a song, but the only tune
she could remember was one her nurse sang, and that was about a frog,
which seemed hardly suitable for the occasion. She considered building a
small castle out of pebbles, but the stones were all damp and crumbly.
Her eyelids grew heavy, and she had almost decided to take a nap when
something extraordinary occurred.

So she was considering in her own mind (as well as she could, for the hot
day made her feel very sleepy and stupid), whether the pleasure of making a
daisy-chain would be worth the trouble of getting up and picking the
daisies, when suddenly a White Rabbit with pink eyes ran close by her.

There was nothing so VERY remarkable in that; nor did Alice think it so
VERY much out of the way to hear the Rabbit say to itself, "Oh dear! Oh
dear! I shall be late!" (when she thought it over afterwards, it occurred
to her that she ought to have wondered at this, but at the time it all
seemed quite natural); but when the Rabbit actually TOOK A WATCH OUT OF
ITS WAISTCOAT-POCKET, and looked at it, and then hurried on, Alice started
to her feet, for it flashed across her mind that she had never before seen
a rabbit with either a waistcoat-pocket, or a watch to take out of it, and
burning with curiosity, she ran across the field after it, and fortunately
was just in time to see it pop down a large rabbit-hole under the hedge.

In another moment down went Alice after it, never once considering how in
the world she was to get out again.

The rabbit-hole went straight on like a tunnel for some way, and then
dipped suddenly down, so suddenly that Alice had not a moment to think
about stopping herself before she found herself falling down a very deep
well.

Either the well was very deep, or she fell very slowly, for she had plenty
of time as she went down to look about her and to wonder what was going to
happen next. First, she tried to look down and make out what she was coming
to, but it was too dark to see anything; then she looked at the sides of
the well, and noticed that they were filled with cupboards and book-shelves;
here and there she saw maps and pictures hung upon pegs. She took down a
jar from one of the shelves as she passed; it was labelled "ORANGE MARMALADE,"
but to her great disappointment it was empty: she did not like to drop the
jar for fear of killing somebody underneath, so managed to put it into one
of the cupboards as she fell past it.

As she continued to fall, she noticed that the cupboards grew more
numerous, and between them were little framed pictures and faded maps
of strange countries she had never heard of. Some of the book-shelves
held volumes so old that their covers had crumbled away entirely, and
she wondered who lived down here that needed so many books. "Perhaps,
she thought, "the Rabbit himself is a great reader, or perhaps this well
belongs to a very learned badger." She passed a small globe of the
world, spinning slowly on its axis, and a curious brass instrument
that looked like a telescope but seemed to be pointed UP, which she
thought very odd indeed. There was also a stuffed owl under a glass
dome, its glass eyes glinting in the dim light. A row of small porcelain
figures stood on one shelf—shepherds and shepherdesses in old-fashioned
dress—and a music box with a tiny dancer on top. A framed sampler had
been stitched with the words "Home Sweet Home" in uneven letters, and
beside it hung a small oil painting of a ship at sea. "Whoever lives
here," thought Alice, "has very peculiar taste in decorations."

"Well!" thought Alice to herself, "after a fall like this, I shall think
nothing of tumbling down stairs! How brave they'll all think me at home!
Why, I wouldn't say anything about it, even if I fell off the top of the
house!" (Which was very likely true.)

Down, down, down. Would the fall NEVER come to an end! "I wonder how many
miles I've fallen by this time?" she said aloud. "I must be getting
somewhere near the centre of the earth. Let me see: that would be four
thousand miles down, I think—" (for, you see, Alice had learnt several
things of this sort in her lessons in the schoolroom, and though this was
not a VERY good opportunity for showing off her knowledge, as there was no
one to listen to her, still it was good practice to say it over) "—yes,
that's about the right distance—but then I wonder what Latitude or
Longitude I've got to?" (Alice had no idea what Latitude was, or Longitude
either, but thought they were nice grand words to say.

She tried to calculate how long she had been falling, but the numbers
would not stay in her head. "Perhaps I am falling slower than I think,"
she reasoned. "Or perhaps the well is deeper than I thought. It might
go all the way down to the middle of the earth, or even beyond! What if I
come out the other side and find myself standing upside-down on the
ceiling of the world?" She practised a little curtsey in the air, just
in case, and then laughed at herself for being so silly. "If I come out
among the Antipathies," she thought, "they will think me a very strange
sort of visitor, tumbling out of the sky in a shower of dust and leaves."
She wondered if the people there would offer her tea, and whether it would
pour upwards into the cups instead of downwards, and whether the sugar
would float or sink. She also wondered about their names—would they be
called Antipathians, or something else entirely?

Presently she began again. "I wonder if I shall fall right THROUGH the
earth! How funny it'll seem to come out among the people that walk with
their heads downward! The Antipathies, I think—" (she was rather glad
there WAS no one listening, this time, as it didn't sound at all the right
word) "—but I shall have to ask them what the name of the country is, you
know. Please, Ma'am, is this New Zealand or Australia?" (and she tried to
curtsey as she spoke—fancy CURTSEYING as you're falling through the air!
Do you think you could manage it?) "And what an ignorant little girl
she'll think me for asking! No, it'll never do to ask: perhaps I shall see
it written up somewhere." She looked around as she fell, hoping to spot
a signpost or a directional marker, but the walls of the well offered
nothing but more cupboards and more books. She did see a notice that read
"BEWARE OF THE FALLING" but it was nailed to a shelf below her and she
had passed it before she could read the rest. "It probably said 'Beware
of the falling books,'" she decided, "which makes very good sense indeed."

Down, down, down. There was nothing else to do, so Alice soon began
talking again. "Dinah'll miss me very much to-night, I should think!"
(Dinah was the cat.) "I hope they'll remember her saucer of milk at
tea-time. Dinah my dear! I wish you were down here with me! There are no
mice in the air, I'm afraid, but you might catch a bat, and that's very
like a mouse, you know. But do cats eat bats, I wonder?" And here Alice
began to get rather sleepy, and went on saying to herself, in a dreamy sort
of way, "Do cats eat bats? Do cats eat bats?" and sometimes, "Do bats eat
cats?" for, you see, as she couldn't answer either question, it didn't
much matter which way she put it. She felt that she was dozing off, and had
just begun to dream that she was walking hand in hand with Dinah, and
saying to her very earnestly, "Now, Dinah, tell me the truth: did you ever
eat a bat?" when suddenly, thump! thump! down she came upon a heap of
sticks and dry leaves, and the fall was over.

The landing was soft enough, cushioned as it was by a thick layer of dried
leaves and twigs that rustled and crackled beneath her weight. For a moment
she lay still, catching her breath and wondering whether she had broken any
bones, but finding herself quite unhurt she scrambled quickly to her feet.
She patted her dress down and checked that her pinafore was still tidy.
The air was dry and dusty, with a strange earthy smell unlike anything
she had known above ground. A faint draught came from somewhere ahead,
carrying with it a smell of old wood and candle wax. She brushed the
debris from her dress and looked about her, trying to get her bearings
in the dim light. The tunnel stretched ahead of her, curving gently out
of sight, with a faint light at the far end that seemed to beckon her
forward.

Alice was not a bit hurt, and she jumped up on to her feet in a moment:
she looked up, but it was all dark overhead; before her was another long
passage, and the White Rabbit was still in sight, hurrying down it. There
was not a moment to be lost: away went Alice like the wind, and was just in
time to hear it say, as it turned a corner, "Oh my ears and whiskers, how
late it's getting!" She was close behind it when she turned the corner, but
the Rabbit was no longer to be seen: she found herself in a long, low hall,
which was lit up by a row of lamps hanging from the roof.

These lamps were not like any she had ever seen before; they gave off a
soft, yellowish glow that flickered gently, as if the flames were alive.
The hall itself was a curious shape—longer than it was wide, with a low
ceiling that seemed to press down upon her. The walls were panelled in
dark wood, and the floor was paved with stone flags worn smooth by
countless footsteps. At the far end of the hall, the lamps cast long
shadows that danced and shifted with every flicker of the flame. The
silence was profound, broken only by the faint hiss of the gas lamps
and the sound of her own breathing. It was, Alice thought, the sort
of place where one might expect to meet a ghost—though she was not
entirely sure she believed in ghosts. The shadows seemed to move of
their own accord, and once she thought she saw something scuttle across
the floor near the far wall, but when she looked again there was nothing.

There were doors all round the hall, but they were all locked; and when
Alice had been all the way down one side and up the other, trying every
door, she walked sadly down the middle, wondering how she was ever to get
out again. Each door she tried was firmly shut, and not one of them would
budge even a fraction of an inch when she pushed against it. Some were
tall and narrow, others were short and broad; a few were arched at the
top, and one was perfectly round, like a porthole. But all of them were
locked fast, and none of the keyholes matched the key she had found. She
counted seventeen doors in all, and tried each one twice, but the result
was the same every time. She kicked one in frustration and immediately
regretted it, for it hurt her toe dreadfully. "It would serve me right,"
she muttered, "if I had to hop about on one foot for the rest of the day."
She sat down on the floor in the middle of the hall, utterly defeated.

Suddenly she came upon a little three-legged table, all made of solid
glass; there was nothing on it except a tiny golden key, and Alice's first
thought was that it might belong to one of the doors of the hall; but,
alas! either the locks were too large, or the key was too small, but at any
rate it would not open any of them. However, on the second time round, she
came upon a low curtain she had not noticed before, and behind it was a
little door about fifteen inches high: she tried the little golden key in
the lock, and to her great delight it fitted!

The key turned smoothly, with a satisfying click, and the little door
swung open. Alice knelt down eagerly and peered through, and what she
saw made her catch her breath. Beyond the door lay a passage, no wider
than a rat-hole, but at the far end of it she could see the most
beautiful garden she had ever imagined. There were beds of brilliant
flowers—scarlet, gold, and deepest blue—and fountains that sparkled
in the sunlight, sending up showers of diamond droplets. She could hear
the faint splash of the water and, somewhere in the distance, the sound
of birds singing. A gravel path wound between the flower beds, and in
the centre of the garden stood a single rose tree heavy with crimson
blooms. Beyond it, through a gap in the trees, she glimpsed what appeared
to be a white palace with gleaming turrets. It was, without a doubt, the
most enchanting sight she had ever beheld.


As she walked towards the little door, the golden key warm in her hand,
she could not help but think about all the strange things that had
happened to her in the past hour. A talking rabbit, a seemingly endless
fall, a hall full of locked doors, a bottle that made her shrink—it was
more adventure than she had had in her entire life put together. "If
Papa could see me now," she thought, "he would hardly believe his eyes."
She smiled at the idea and quickened her pace, her small feet pattering
on the cold stone floor.

Alice opened the door and found that it led into a small passage, not much
larger than a rat-hole: she knelt down and looked along the passage into
the loveliest garden you ever saw. How she longed to get out of that dark
hall, and wander about among those beds of bright flowers and those cool
fountains, but she could not even get her head through the doorway; "and
even if my head would go through," thought poor Alice, "it would be of very
little use without my shoulders. Oh, how I wish I could shut up like a
telescope! I think I could, if I only knew how to begin." For, you see, so
many out-of-the-way things had happened lately, that Alice had begun to
think that very few things indeed were really impossible.

The sunlight in the garden seemed to dance and shimmer, as if the whole
place were alive with joy. Alice could make out stately elm trees in the
distance, and beyond them what appeared to be a line of rolling hills,
soft and blue in the haze. She could almost smell the flowers, and the
sound of the fountains—a cool, musical splashing—made her feel quite
faint with longing. She pressed her face as close to the opening as she
could, but the passage was too narrow for even her head to pass through
by more than a few inches. She pulled back with a sigh and looked at the
little door, now closed again, with the saddest expression imaginable.

There seemed to be no use in waiting by the little door, so she went back
to the table, half hoping she might find another key on it, or at any rate
a book of rules for shutting people up like telescopes: this time she found
a little bottle on it, ("which certainly was not here before," said Alice,)
and round the neck of the bottle was a paper label, with the words "DRINK
ME" beautifully printed on it in large letters.

The bottle was made of a curious pale blue glass that seemed to glow
faintly from within. The label around its neck was tied with a fine silk
ribbon, and the letters of the inscription were picked out in gold. It was
unquestionably the most inviting thing she had ever seen, and yet Alice
hesitated, turning the bottle over and over in her hands. She held it up
to the lamplight and watched the liquid inside shimmer and shift like
liquid starlight. She uncorked it and sniffed cautiously; the aroma was
a delightful mixture of fruit and flowers and something indefinable that
made her mouth water. There was a faint fizzing sound from within, as
of tiny bubbles rising to the surface.

It was all very well to say "Drink me," but the wise little Alice was not
going to do THAT in a hurry. "No, I'll look first," she said, "and see
whether it's marked 'poison' or not"; for she had read several nice little
histories about children who had got burnt, and eaten up by wild beasts and
other unpleasant things, all because they WOULD not remember the simple
rules their friends had taught them: such as, that a red-hot poker will
burn you if you hold it too long; and that if you cut your finger VERY
deeply with a knife, it usually bleeds; and she had never forgotten that,
if you drink much from a bottle marked "poison," it is almost certain to
disagree with you, sooner or later. She also remembered a story about a
little girl who turned into a sheep after drinking from a mysterious flask,
though she could not recall whether that had ended well or badly. "Not
that I should become a sheep," she told herself, "for there are no
scissors down here to shear me if I did." And she giggled at the
thought of herself as a woolly sheep, bleating in a corner.

She turned the bottle this way and that, watching how the light played
through the blue glass. The liquid within was not quite still; it seemed
to move of its own accord, swirling in lazy spirals. Tiny sparkles of
light danced within it, like stars trapped in a miniature universe. The
cork, when she finally pulled it free, made a satisfying pop, and the
scent that rose from the bottle was so delightful that Alice felt her
resolve weakening. She held it away from her and gave herself a stern
lecture about the importance of caution. "Remember what happened to the
girl who ate the wrong mushroom in that story Auntie read," she warned
herself. But she could not remember how that particular story ended,
which made the warning somewhat less effective.

However, this bottle was NOT marked "poison," so Alice ventured to taste
it, and finding it very nice, (it had, in fact, a sort of mixed flavour of
cherry-tart, custard, pine-apple, roast turkey, toffee, and hot buttered
toast,) she very soon finished it off. She licked her lips and set the
empty bottle down on the table with a satisfied sigh. The taste lingered
pleasantly on her tongue, reminding her of Christmas morning and birthday
parties and all the nicest things she could think of. "If I ever write a
cookery book," she thought, "I shall put that flavour in everything."
And she patted her stomach, which felt pleasantly warm.

"What a curious feeling!" said Alice; "I must be shutting up like a
telescope."

And so it was indeed: she was now only ten inches high, and her face
brightened up at the thought that she was now the right size for going
through the little door into that lovely garden. First, however, she waited
for a few minutes to see if she was going to shrink any further: she felt a
little nervous about this; "for it might end, you know," said Alice to
herself, "in my going out altogether, like a candle. I wonder what I
should be like then?" And she tried to fancy what the flame of a candle is
like after the candle is blown out, for she could not remember ever having
seen such a thing. The sensation was not unpleasant—a gentle, tingling
feeling that started at the top of her head and spread downwards through
her entire body, as if she were being softly compressed by invisible hands.
She watched her reflection in the glass table-top, fascinated, as it
grew smaller and smaller until she was no bigger than a mouse. Her dress,
which had fit perfectly moments before, now hung loosely around her, and
she had to gather up the fabric to keep from tripping over it. "I shall
need a new wardrobe," she thought, "if I am going to stay this size."
She took a few experimental steps, finding that walking in her now-oversized
dress required a great deal of concentration to avoid entanglement.

After a while, finding that nothing more happened, she decided on going
into the garden at once; but, alas for poor Alice! when she got to the
door, she found she had forgotten the little golden key, and when she went
back to the table for it, she found she could not possibly reach it: she
could see it quite plainly through the glass, and she tried her best to
climb up one of the legs of the table, but it was too slippery; and when
she had tired herself out with trying, the poor little thing sat down and
cried.

The table leg was perfectly smooth—no handhold, no notch, nothing to grip.
She tried jumping, but at ten inches high she could not leap anywhere near
the top. She even attempted to shove the table across the floor and tip it
over, but it was far too heavy for her tiny arms. She looked around for
a stick or a stone to throw at the key to knock it off the table, but
the hall was swept perfectly clean. She thought about calling for help,
but there was no one to hear her. She even tried blowing at the key,
hoping a strong gust might dislodge it, but it did not so much as tremble.
At last, exhausted and frustrated, she sank down in a heap and let the
tears flow freely. All that lovely garden, so close and yet so impossibly
far away, with the golden key gleaming mockingly on the table above her!
It was too cruel.

"Come, there's no use in crying like that!" said Alice to herself, rather
sharply; "I advise you to leave off this minute!" She generally gave
herself very good advice, (though she very seldom followed it), and
sometimes she scolded herself so severely as to bring tears into her eyes;
and once she remembered trying to box her own ears for having cheated
herself in a game of croquet she was playing against herself, for this
curious child was very fond of pretending to be two people. "But it's no
use now," thought poor Alice, "to pretend to be two people! Why, there's
hardly enough of me left to make ONE respectable person!" She cried even
harder at this thought, wetting the stone floor with her tears until a
small puddle formed around her. "Crying won't get the key any closer,"
she told herself sternly, but the tears kept coming all the same.

Soon her eye fell on a little glass box that was lying under the table:
she opened it, and found in it a very small cake, on which the words "EAT
ME" were beautifully marked in currants. "Well, I'll eat it," said Alice,
"and if it makes me grow larger, I can reach the key; and if it makes me
grow smaller, I can creep under the door; so either way I'll get into the
garden, and I don't care which happens!" She dried her eyes on her sleeve
and picked up the cake with renewed hope.

The cake was small and round, no bigger than a shilling, and the currants
that spelled out its message were dark and sweet-smelling. Alice broke off
a tiny crumb and put it cautiously into her mouth. Nothing happened. She
ate a little more. Still nothing. She began to wonder if the cake was a
fraud, or if perhaps the magic only worked when you ate the whole thing
at once. She sniffed it suspiciously, but it smelled perfectly ordinary—
like a plain seed-cake, such as Cook made at home on baking days. She
took another bite, then another, chewing slowly and waiting for some
change to come over her. "Perhaps," she thought, "the effect takes a
few minutes to work, like the cough mixture Nurse gives me." She waited,
counting under her breath, but still nothing changed. "Well," she said
at last, "I may as well finish it. It tastes very nice, even if it does
nothing at all." She took another bite, then another, enjoying the sweet,
buttery flavour and the pop of the currants between her teeth.

She ate a little bit, and said anxiously to herself, "Which way? Which
way?", holding her hand on the top of her head to feel which way it was
growing, and she was quite surprised to find that she remained the same
size: to be sure, this generally happens when one eats cake, but Alice had
got so much into the way of expecting nothing but out-of-the-way things to
happen, that it seemed quite dull and stupid for life to go on in the
common way.

So she set to work, and very soon finished off the cake.
