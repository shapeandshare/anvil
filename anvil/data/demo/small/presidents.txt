## George Washington — First Annual Message (January 8, 1790)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

I embrace with great satisfaction the opportunity which now presents itself of congratulating you on the present favorable prospects of our public affairs. The recent accession of the important state of North Carolina to the Constitution of the United States, of which official information has been received, the rising credit and respectability of our country, the general and increasing good will toward the government of the Union, and the concord, peace, and plenty with which we are blessed, are circumstances auspicious in an eminent degree to our national prosperity.

In rendering to the Supreme Being the tribute of our sincere gratitude for these and numerous other blessings which we have been permitted to enjoy, it becomes us likewise to offer our earnest supplications that He will be graciously pleased to continue to us the enjoyment of them; and that He will be pleased to impart to the national councils such wisdom, firmness, and moderation as may be expected to direct our deliberations in the most desirable direction.

In the discharge of my official duties I shall endeavor to recommend to your attention such matters as appear to me to be essential to the welfare of our common country. Among these, the advancement of agriculture, commerce, and manufactures, by all proper means, will not escape your consideration. Nor can I omit to recommend a special attention to the establishment of a uniform system of militia, and to the organization of the executive departments.

The proper establishment of the public credit is of infinite importance to our national character and to the security and welfare of the people. I recommend to your serious consideration the adoption of such measures as shall effectually promote this great end. The adoption of a proper system for the management of the public revenue, the regulation of the currency, and the encouragement of domestic manufactures are objects which must naturally claim your attention.

I cannot conclude without mentioning how essential it is to the happiness of the people that the principles of government should be well understood, and that the general welfare should be the constant object of our pursuit. Let us therefore unite our efforts in promoting the great interests of our country, and in establishing those wise and salutary measures which are calculated to secure the liberties and promote the happiness of the American people.

Wherein it may be necessary to augment the public revenue, I trust that it will be done in such a manner as to bear as lightly as possible upon the people, and to promote the general welfare in the most effectual manner. The situation of our country, the fertility of our soil, the industry of our citizens, and the extent of our commerce, all concur to afford a reasonable prospect that the burdens which are necessary for the support of government may be borne without oppression.

There are several other matters which I shall take occasion to communicate to you during the session, and I rely with confidence upon your wisdom and patriotism for the adoption of such measures as the public good may require. The Constitution of the United States having become the supreme law of the land, it is our duty to render it the basis of all our proceedings, and to cultivate that harmony of sentiment and unity of action which are essential to the success of our common efforts.

## George Washington — Second Annual Message (December 8, 1790)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

It gives me particular satisfaction to inform you that the revenue of the United States has progressively increased, and that the public credit is acquiring that degree of confidence which is necessary for the prosperity of our nation. The receipts into the Treasury have been such as to justify a well-founded hope that the resources of the country are fully adequate to meeting the engagements of the government.

The arrangements which have been made for the establishment of a national bank, and for the funding of the public debt, have contributed essentially to the restoration of public credit. The confidence which the people of the United States have shown in the measures of government is a most pleasing testimony of their wisdom and patriotism.

I have directed the proper officers to lay before you such information as may enable you to judge of the state of the Union and to adopt such measures as the public good may require. The protection and encouragement of our commerce, the improvement of our agriculture, and the advancement of our manufactures are objects which cannot fail to engage your attention.

The Indian tribes who inhabit our western frontiers have manifested a disposition for peace which it is desirable to cultivate. Measures have been taken to establish treaties with them, and to secure the blessings of peace to our citizens who reside in those remote regions.

## George Washington — Third Annual Message (October 25, 1791)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

I meet you upon the present occasion with feelings of satisfaction at the prosperous condition of our country. The various branches of the public service have been conducted with efficiency, and the affairs of the Union are in a state of progressive improvement.

The success of the measures which have been adopted for the establishment of public credit is now fully demonstrated. The value of our public securities has greatly increased, and the confidence of our citizens in the justice and wisdom of their government has been strengthened.

I recommend to your consideration the adoption of such measures as may be necessary for the encouragement of domestic manufactures, and for the promotion of agriculture and commerce. The establishment of military academies, and the proper organization of the militia of the United States, are objects which merit your serious attention.

The progress of the population and settlement of our country has been remarkable. New states have been added to the Union, and the foundation of future prosperity has been laid upon a broad and solid basis. It is with great satisfaction that I contemplate the flourishing condition of our country and the prospect of its future greatness.

I cannot omit to mention the importance of establishing uniform rules of naturalization, and of providing for the uniform administration of justice throughout the United States. These objects are essential to the harmony and prosperity of the Union, and they deserve your careful consideration.

## George Washington — Fourth Annual Message (November 6, 1792)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

It is with heartfelt satisfaction that I am again able to congratulate you on the flourishing state of our country. The abundant harvests which have blessed our land, the increasing prosperity of our commerce, and the general improvement of our condition are subjects of grateful acknowledgement to the Author of all Good.

The revenue of the United States continues to be productive, and the public debt is being gradually reduced. The confidence which the people have placed in the measures of government has been fully justified by the success which has attended them.

Peace continues to prevail with the Indian tribes, and our western frontiers enjoy a state of tranquility which is essential to their prosperity. The arrangements which have been made for the protection of our frontiers have been attended with satisfactory results.

I cannot recommend too strongly to your consideration the adoption of measures for the promotion of science and literature. Knowledge is in every country the surest basis of public happiness. In one in which the measures of government receive their impression so immediately from the sense of the community as in ours, it is proportionably essential.

## George Washington — Fifth Annual Message (December 3, 1793)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

Since the commencement of the present year, the principal events which have occurred in the affairs of the United States have been connected with the situation of our foreign relations. The war which has broken out among the principal powers of Europe has necessarily involved considerations of great importance to the interests of our country.

Upon this occasion, I have thought it proper to issue a proclamation of neutrality, enjoining upon all citizens of the United States a conduct conformable to the laws and to the duties of neutrality. It is my earnest desire that the United States may remain at peace with all nations, and that we may cultivate that friendly intercourse which is essential to our prosperity.

The preservation of peace with the Indian tribes has been an object of particular attention. Treaties have been formed with several tribes, and measures have been taken to secure the tranquility of our frontiers.

I recommend to your consideration the adoption of such measures as may be necessary for the augmentation of our military force, and for the proper defense of our country. The protection of our commerce against the depredations of foreign cruisers, and the security of our seamen, are objects which demand your attention.

## George Washington — Sixth Annual Message (November 19, 1794)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

When we call to mind the recent insurrection which has taken place in the western parts of Pennsylvania, it becomes our duty to reflect upon the importance of maintaining the authority of the laws. The insurrection was the first of its kind, and it is to be hoped that it will be the last.

I am happy to inform you that the insurrection has been suppressed without the loss of much blood, and that the authority of the laws has been fully maintained. The prompt and decisive measures which were adopted for this purpose have had a salutary effect in demonstrating the strength of the government.

The negotiations which have been carried on with Great Britain have resulted in a treaty which I shall lay before you for your consideration. It is my earnest hope that this treaty may contribute to the establishment of a friendly and beneficial intercourse between the two countries.

The state of our finances continues to be favorable. The revenue has been productive, and the public credit is well established. The gradual reduction of the public debt is proceeding in a manner which is satisfactory.

## George Washington — Seventh Annual Message (December 8, 1795)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

The period of a new year is a proper occasion for looking back upon the past and for looking forward to the future. The retrospect is, upon the whole, highly satisfactory. The United States have been blessed with peace, plenty, and prosperity.

The treaty with Great Britain having been ratified, it is to be hoped that the differences which have subsisted between the two countries will be adjusted, and that a friendly intercourse may be established. The treaty contains stipulations which are calculated to promote the mutual interests of both nations.

I recommend to your attention the state of the military establishment and the necessity of making suitable provision for the defense of our frontiers. The protection of our citizens from the incursions of hostile Indians, and the preservation of peace with the various tribes, are objects of importance.

The encouragement of agriculture, commerce, and manufactures, by all proper means, cannot be too strongly recommended. The prosperity of our country depends upon the industry and enterprise of our citizens.

## George Washington — Eighth Annual Message (December 7, 1796)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

In receiving you at the opening of the present session of Congress, I cannot but express the satisfaction I feel in seeing the affairs of the Union in a state of progressive improvement. The period of my service in the office of President being now drawing to a close, it is natural that I should look back upon the events which have occurred during my administration.

The state of the public credit continues to be good. The revenue of the United States has been productive, and the public debt has been reduced. The confidence of the people in the measures of government has been fully justified by the results.

The relations with the several Indian tribes have been generally pacific, and measures have been taken to promote civilization among them and to secure them in the possession of their lands.

I have thought it proper to communicate to you the state of our foreign relations, and to recommend such measures as may be necessary for the security and prosperity of the United States. The interests of our country require that we should maintain a respectable military force, and that we should cultivate peace and friendship with all nations.

In taking my leave of you, I cannot omit to express my earnest wishes that the measures which have been adopted during my administration may be conducive to the happiness and prosperity of the American people. May the blessings of Heaven be showered upon this country, and may the wisdom and virtue of its citizens continue to secure the liberties and promote the welfare of the United States.

## John Adams — First Annual Message (November 22, 1797)

Gentlemen of the Senate and Gentlemen of the House of Representatives:

I was pleased to observe, in the communications which I have received from the several states, a strong and general attachment to the Constitution of the United States, and a firm determination to support the government. The prosperity of our country has been increasing, and the blessings of peace have been enjoyed.

The state of our relations with foreign powers has demanded serious attention. The negotiations with France have not yet been brought to a satisfactory conclusion, and it becomes necessary that we should adopt measures for the protection of our commerce and the security of our citizens.

I recommend to your consideration the adoption of such measures as may be necessary for the augmentation of our naval force. The protection of our commerce on the high seas, and the defense of our coasts, require that we should have a respectable navy.

The state of the public finances is favorable. The revenue has been productive, and the public debt is being gradually reduced. The credit of the United States stands high among the nations of the world.

## John Adams — Second Annual Message (December 8, 1798)

Gentlemen of the Senate and Gentlemen of the House of Representatives:

While I congratulate you on the general prosperity of our country, I cannot but express my deep concern at the state of our relations with the French Republic. The refusal of that government to receive our minister, and the aggressive measures which have been pursued against our commerce, have rendered it necessary that we should prepare for the defense of our country.

I have directed the proper officers to take measures for the protection of our commerce and the defense of our coasts. The augmentation of our naval force has been commenced, and preparations have been made for the organization of a provisional army.

The conduct of our citizens during the present crisis has been such as to inspire confidence. The spirit of patriotism which has been manifested among all classes of our people is a sure pledge that the liberties and independence of our country will be maintained.

The measures which have been adopted for the establishment of a navy have been attended with success. Several vessels have been launched, and others are in preparation. It is my earnest hope that the United States may possess a naval force sufficient to protect their commerce and to command respect among the nations of the world.

## John Adams — Third Annual Message (December 3, 1799)

Gentlemen of the Senate and Gentlemen of the House of Representatives:

It is with great satisfaction that I am again enabled to congratulate you on the continued prosperity of our country. The blessings of peace, under the favor of Divine Providence, have been continued to us, and the various branches of the public service have been conducted with efficiency.

The negotiations with France have been renewed, and I am happy to inform you that there is a prospect of an accommodation of the differences which have subsisted between the two countries. Commissioners have been appointed on both sides, and it is hoped that a treaty may be concluded which will restore harmony and friendship.

The revenue of the United States continues to be productive, and the public debt is being reduced in accordance with the provisions of law. The credit of the government stands high, and the confidence of the people in the wisdom of their rulers has been maintained.

The state of our military and naval establishments is such as to afford reasonable security against foreign aggression. The measures which have been adopted for the defense of our country have been attended with satisfactory results.

## Thomas Jefferson — First Annual Message (December 8, 1801)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

It is a circumstance of sincere gratification that I am enabled to inform you of the continued prosperity of our country under the auspices of peace. The hand of Providence has been manifestly extended in our favor, and we have been blessed with abundance.

The several great departments of the government have been administered with economy and efficiency. The necessary expenses of the government have been reduced, and the public debt has been diminished. It is my earnest desire that the energies of the government should be directed to the preservation of peace, the promotion of agriculture and commerce, and the encouragement of the arts and sciences.

The relations with the Indian tribes have been cultivated, and measures have been taken to promote their civilization and to secure them in the possession of their lands. The policy of the government toward the Indian tribes has been one of justice and humanity.

The encouragement of agriculture is of primary importance to the prosperity of our country. The diffusion of knowledge among the people, and the promotion of education, are objects which cannot be too strongly recommended. No nation can long remain free which does not possess a virtuous and enlightened citizenry.

I cannot conclude without recommending to your consideration the necessity of a revision of the judiciary system, and the adoption of such measures as may be necessary for its improvement. The administration of justice is the foundation of civil society.

While we are endeavoring to secure the blessings of peace at home, it is also proper that we should cultivate peace and friendship with all nations. The United States has ever held the principle that the ocean is free to all, and that commerce should be unmolested in time of war. We wish to be on friendly terms with every power, and to trade with all. Under this policy, our navigation has increased with rapidity, and our commercial cities have risen to a state of unexampled prosperity.

Few things are more important to the preservation of republican government than a watchful and efficient administration of the laws. The people of the United States have shown by their conduct that they are capable of self-government, and that they know how to value the blessings which flow from a well-regulated system of liberty and law.

## Thomas Jefferson — Second Annual Message (December 15, 1802)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

When our attention is called to the state of our country, we find abundant reason for gratitude and satisfaction. The blessings of peace have been continued to us, and the various interests of the nation have been steadily advancing.

The cession of Louisiana by France to Spain, and the subsequent transfer of that important territory to the French Republic, are events which demand our serious attention. The possession of New Orleans at the mouth of the Mississippi is of great importance to the commerce of the western states, and every effort will be made to secure the rights and interests of the United States in that region.

The finances of the United States continue in a highly prosperous condition. The revenue has exceeded the expenditures, and the public debt has been further reduced. It is my earnest wish that the extinguishment of the public debt may be pursued with vigor, and that the burdens of taxation may be lightened as far as the public interest will permit.

The measures which have been adopted for the encouragement of the militia have been attended with beneficial effects. The defense of our country rests upon the virtue and patriotism of our citizens.

## Thomas Jefferson — Third Annual Message (October 17, 1803)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

It gives me great satisfaction to inform you that the acquisition of Louisiana has been accomplished by treaty with the French Republic. This important addition to our territory secures to the United States the free navigation of the Mississippi, and opens an immense expanse of fertile country to the enterprise of our citizens.

The treaty has been ratified by the Senate, and possession of the territory has been taken. The government of the territory has been organized in a manner calculated to promote the interests of its inhabitants and to secure the rights of the United States.

The boundaries of the territory acquired are extensive, embracing the whole of that vast region which lies between the Mississippi and the Rocky Mountains, and extending from the Gulf of Mexico to the northern limits of the continent. This acquisition has been accomplished by peaceful negotiation, and at a moderate expense, and it secures to the United States advantages of incalculable value.

The finances of the country continue in a flourishing condition. The revenue has been abundant, and the public debt has been further reduced. The prospect of the speedy extinguishment of the public debt is a subject of great satisfaction.

The encouragement of the arts and sciences, the promotion of education, and the diffusion of knowledge among the people are objects which cannot be too strongly recommended to the attention of the national legislature.

I also recommend to your consideration the establishment of a system of public roads and canals, for the purpose of connecting the various parts of our extensive country and of facilitating the transportation of the products of our industry. The improvement of the means of communication is essential to the prosperity of the Union.

## Thomas Jefferson — Fourth Annual Message (November 8, 1804)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

To a people, fellow-citizens, who are in the full enjoyment of prosperity and happiness, nothing can be more grateful than to be assured that their government continues to be administered with wisdom and integrity. I am happy to inform you that the public affairs of the United States continue in a state of progressive improvement.

The acquisition of Louisiana has been followed by the organization of its government and the establishment of such institutions as are best calculated to promote the happiness and prosperity of its inhabitants. The exploration of the western country has been prosecuted under the direction of the government, and important discoveries have been made.

The revenue of the United States continues to be productive, and the public debt has been still further reduced. The reduction of taxes has been accomplished, and the burdens of the people have been lightened.

The relations with the Indian tribes have been conducted on principles of justice and humanity. The policy of the government has been to preserve peace with all the tribes, and to promote their civilization and improvement.

## Thomas Jefferson — Fifth Annual Message (December 3, 1805)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

It is with great satisfaction that I am again enabled to congratulate you on the continued prosperity and happiness of our country. The blessings of peace have been enjoyed, and the various interests of the nation have been steadily promoted.

The expedition which was sent to explore the western country has returned, and the information which has been collected is of the highest importance. The Missouri River has been ascended to its source, and the Columbia River has been descended to the Pacific Ocean. This discovery opens new prospects for the extension of our commerce and the diffusion of our population.

The finances of the country continue to be prosperous. The revenue has been adequate to the expenditures, and the public debt has been further reduced. The measures which have been adopted for the security of our commerce and the protection of our seamen have been attended with success.

I recommend to your consideration the adoption of such measures as may be necessary for the improvement of our harbors and the construction of roads and canals. The internal improvement of our country is an object of great importance to its future prosperity.

## Thomas Jefferson — Sixth Annual Message (December 2, 1806)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

The state of our country continues to be prosperous and happy. We have been blessed with peace, and the various branches of the public service have been conducted with efficiency.

The information which has been received from our ministers abroad affords ground for hope that the differences which have subsisted with foreign powers may be adjusted by negotiation. It is my earnest desire that the United States may continue to cultivate peace and friendship with all nations.

The revenue of the United States has been productive beyond expectation, and the public debt has been reduced to a sum which promises its early extinguishment. The prosperity of our finances is a subject of great satisfaction.

I consider it my duty to recommend to your consideration the adoption of a constitutional amendment providing for the exclusion of the slave trade after the year 1808. The Constitution has provided that Congress may prohibit the importation of slaves after that date, and it becomes us to exercise this power in a manner consistent with the principles of humanity and justice.

## Thomas Jefferson — Seventh Annual Message (October 27, 1807)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

The circumstances under which we meet are of a nature to excite deep concern. The aggression which has been committed upon the Chesapeake, one of our national vessels, by a British ship of war, has produced a state of things which demands the most serious consideration.

I have caused a proclamation to be issued interdicting the entrance of British armed vessels into our harbors, and I have taken measures for the defense of our coasts. The conduct of the British government in this affair has been such as to justify the expectation of reparation.

The embargo which has been laid upon our vessels and upon the exportation of our produce is a measure of precaution which the public safety requires. It is to be hoped that this measure may induce the belligerent powers to respect our rights and to acknowledge the justice of our claims.

The finances of the country are in a state of prosperity. The revenue has been productive, and the public debt has been further reduced. The resources of the United States are fully adequate to meet any emergency which may arise.

## Thomas Jefferson — Eighth Annual Message (November 8, 1808)

Fellow-Citizens of the Senate and House of Representatives:

The period of my service in the office of President being now about to close, it is with satisfaction that I look back upon the events which have occurred during my administration. The United States have been blessed with prosperity, and the great interests of the nation have been steadily promoted.

The embargo which was laid upon our commerce has been a subject of great discussion. It was a measure of precaution, adopted in a time of difficulty, and it has been executed with fidelity. The effects of the embargo have been felt by our own citizens as well as by foreign nations.

The revenue of the United States has been reduced by the operation of the embargo, but the public credit remains unimpaired. The resources of our country are abundant, and the spirit of our people is unbroken.

The encouragement of agriculture, commerce, and manufactures, the promotion of education, and the diffusion of knowledge among the people, are objects which cannot be too strongly recommended to the attention of the national legislature. In taking my leave of the station which I have so long occupied, I offer my sincere wishes for the happiness and prosperity of the American people.
